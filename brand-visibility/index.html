We have the narrative. We have the model. These are the organizational moves that turn them into an operating reality — starting with our own teams.

1
Redefine what the content team does
The content team becomes a research and editorial intelligence function. Its primary output is validated thinking and practical tools — not articles. Articles are one expression of that thinking.

The intelligence function produces three layers of output:

Core Insight

The thesis, original finding, or unique argument. Developed through research and expert validation. This is what makes the content worth creating

Operational Tools

Templates, checklists, spreadsheets, playbooks, decision frameworks. The hands-on assets people actually download, share, and use. Often the thing that travels furthest

Content Expressions

Article, video, webinar, podcast, social. The formats that teach the insight and distribute the tools. Each channel does what it does best

2
Invest deep, then sustain distribution
Instead of spreading thin effort across dozens of topics, concentrate investment on fewer ideas — then distribute each one across every relevant surface for as long as it takes to build salience.

Invest

Research, expert validation, framework development. Front-load the effort into something with a genuine core insight and practical tools people can use immediately

Incubate

Long-form writing as the incubation ground. Writing forces clarity, exposes gaps, refines the argument. The article is where the idea gets sharp — the beginning of the lifecycle, not the end

Distribute & Sustain

One idea, many expressions — distributed across surfaces and sustained over months. Podcasts, conferences, guest pieces, social, partnerships. Repetition builds salience. Keep going until the market associates the topic with your brand

Launch & publish Podcast & conference circuit Salience threshold

Most content teams publish, promote for a week, and move on. That wastes the investment. Artists tour the same material for months or years — adapting it to the stage, but repeating the core ideas until their name is synonymous with the topic. James Clear has been touring Atomic Habits for five years. The content calendar shifts from "what are we publishing this week?" to "how are we still touring our core ideas this quarter?"

The shift in planning

The strategist sets the distribution map at the planning stage, not after publication. Before a word is written, the team knows: which formats will express this idea, which channels will distribute it, which partnerships and appearances will sustain it, and for how long. The question changes from "what should we publish next?" to "what's worth investing in that we can distribute for the next 3-6 months?"

3
Give every team better ammunition
The intelligence function doesn't impose a new objective on distribution teams. It gives every team — PR, social, partnerships, influencers — something genuinely worth working with, aligned to a coherent narrative.

Current reality

Content team publishes an article. Distribution teams receive it after the fact. Each team adapts it for their channel with limited context. Message fragments. Effort is duplicated. Nothing compounds

With this model

Intelligence function produces a researched, validated idea with operational tools and a distribution map. Every team gets ammunition designed for their channel's strengths and their existing goals. Coherence via entity brief creates the visibility outcome across all surfaces

Why this gets buy-in

PR wants coverage? Give them a framework worth covering. Social wants engagement? Give them an insight worth discussing. Partnerships want co-marketing? Give them research worth co-branding. Each team pursues its own goals. The aggregate effect — brand visibility across every surface, including AI — is the emergent outcome of coherent distribution, not a mandate imposed from above

4
Fix measurement
Teams measured on channel-specific revenue will optimize for channel-specific outcomes. The measurement has to change before the behavior can.

Now

Channel-attributed MRR. Each team owns their number. Content that travels beyond its origin channel gets no credit for the team that created it

Transition

Origination tracking + fractional contribution alongside existing metrics. When a flagship idea becomes a video, a webinar, and three emails — record the lineage, share the credit

Target

Shared brand visibility score. Individual channel metrics still tracked, but the primary KPI everyone shares is the aggregate visibility outcome across all surfaces

Every team's work contributes to different components. Content and SEO drive owned property performance and AI visibility. Social drives channel presence. PR and community efforts drive media citations and third-party mentions. Branded search volume is the ultimate shared outcome — people searching for the brand by name. The single score makes the aggregate effect visible. Individual teams still track channel metrics, but the score connects everyone's work to a shared result.
Where does paid fit? Paid amplification is an accelerant across the score, not a separate component. It can boost any surface — promote a flagship article, amplify a social post, distribute a tool. The score measures visibility outcomes regardless of whether the driver was organic or paid. Paid spend shows up in how fast the components move, not as its own slice.
5
Centralize AI-powered production
The effort inversion only works if production gets radically faster. Right now, AI adoption is fragmented — individual creators using tools independently. That needs to become shared infrastructure.

What this enables

React to trends in days instead of weeks. Produce multiple format expressions from a single idea without proportional effort. Compress the timeline from validated insight to multi-channel distribution

What this requires

A content-experienced person who builds specific AI workflows for the team — reusable prompts, format adaptation pipelines, research synthesis tools. Centralizes what's currently scattered. Distinct from project management: a PM manages deadlines and production flow; this role builds the engine that makes production faster

Key role evolutions

Two roles that make this operational

Content Strategist

Evolved role

From keyword researcher and brief writer to the person who architects how a validated idea reaches the audience across every relevant surface — and sustains distribution over months.

  • Identify opportunities from the entity brief and market landscape — not just keyword demand
  • Determine the core insight and whether it warrants sustained, multi-format investment
  • Set the full distribution map at the planning stage: formats, channels, partnerships, timeline
  • Collaborate with writers and editors to develop the thinking and operational tools (templates, playbooks, checklists)
  • Coordinate with distribution teams before creation, not after publication
  • Manage expert relationships and bring outside perspectives into the intelligence function
  • Own the sustained distribution plan: podcast pitches, conference submissions, ongoing social amplification

Content Operations Lead

New role

Owns the AI-powered production layer. Builds the shared infrastructure that makes the effort inversion possible at team scale — not individual experimentation, but centralized capability.

  • Audit current AI usage across the team — identify what's working, standardize it, share it
  • Build reusable workflows for content production: research synthesis, draft generation, format adaptation
  • Own the pipeline from single intelligence asset to multiple format expressions
  • Connect AI workflows with creative tools and the team's production systems
  • Track time-from-idea-to-distribution. Identify bottlenecks. Build automation to remove them
  • Quality control for AI-generated output — ensure entity brief alignment across all adapted formats
